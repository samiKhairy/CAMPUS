\section{What this follows up on}

This report is the third milestone in our evaluation of the bidirectional device--edge communication layer for the CAMPUS project. 
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Report 1 (May 26)} benchmarked gRPC, Zenoh, and MQTT over TCP at small scales ($N \le 10$) on a local laptop, discovering that MQTT over TCP collapses under packet loss due to broker-side TCP head-of-line blocking.
    \item \textbf{Report 2 (May 29)} introduced QUIC transports (Zenoh-over-QUIC and MQTT-over-QUIC) and scaled the device sweep up to $N=50$ on the local Windows/WSL2 host. It demonstrated that swapping TCP for QUIC completely rescued MQTT, but exposed local VM-induced resource limits (CPU scheduling starvation and virtual network bridge drops) that contaminated results at $N \ge 20$.
\end{enumerate}

To isolate true protocol limits from host-virtualization artifacts, we moved the benchmarking suite to the Pisa lab's high-performance bare-metal compute server (\texttt{braine-head-node}, 112 threads, 251~GB RAM). We executed the full 360-experiment matrix natively on Linux Docker CE. This report documents the bare-metal scalability verification and details a newly identified QUIC client-side deadlock under maximum load.

\section{Infrastructure Delta: Host vs. Server}

The compute capacity of the Braine node removes the CPU and memory contention that plagued the $N=50$ laptop runs. More importantly, native Linux Docker CE routes network traffic directly through native Linux kernel namespaces, bypassing the double-translation overhead of the WSL2/Docker Desktop hypervisor network switch.

This change in the network path has a massive effect on baseline latencies and packet handling:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Hypervisor Overhead Elimination:} Local bridge RTT baseline drops from $\sim$2.6~ms to $\sim$0.5~ms, revealing a static $\sim$2~ms virtualization latency added by the laptop's VM switch.
    \item \textbf{Scheduler Stability:} The 112-thread capacity eliminates host CPU starvation, allowing containerized clients to publish at precise intervals (5~Hz / 10~Hz) without timing drift, even at $N=50$.
\end{itemize}

With virtualization delays eliminated, the protocols operate at bare-metal speeds. Zenoh-QUIC and Zenoh (TCP) are exceptionally fast, both sitting at $\sim$0.5~ms median. MQTT-over-QUIC (NanoSDK C client with asynchronous callbacks) operates at a baseline RTT of **1.14~ms**, confirming that the 45~ms floor observed in early TCP runs was entirely client-side polling delay from the Python \texttt{paho-mqtt} loop, not an MQTT protocol limitation.

At $N=50$ on the clean network, Zenoh and Zenoh-QUIC scale cleanly, keeping their p95 latencies below **2~ms**. MQTT-QUIC exhibits minor multiplexing overhead, rising to a p95 of $\sim$16~ms at $N=50$.

At $N=10$, the server's resource abundance completely solves gRPC's packet loss issue, dropping it from 11.1\% on the laptop to **0.3\%** on the server. This proves that the previous packet loss at $N=10$ was a local CPU scheduling artifact, not a protocol limitation.

However, at $N=20$, the protocol-intrinsic limits emerge:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{gRPC HTTP/2 Stream Exhaustion:} gRPC drops exactly **50.2\%** of messages at $N=20$. The latency of successful packets remains near the network floor ($\sim$41~ms), but half are lost. This confirms that gRPC's HTTP/2 multiplexing reaches a hard connection/thread concurrency ceiling under simultaneous fan-out.
    \item \textbf{MQTT TCP Queue Congestion:} On the server, the queue collapse of MQTT over TCP is even more pronounced: median RTT at $N=20$ rises to **3.1 seconds** (up from 460~ms on the laptop). Because the server's compute power allows the clients to publish at a steady, unthrottled 10~Hz, the broker queue accumulates messages faster and collapses earlier.
\end{itemize}

* **MQTT-QUIC at $N=10$:** Registers a **212.52~ms median** under degraded 5G conditions. Compared to the **34.9 seconds** of MQTT TCP, this represents a **164$\times$ latency reduction** achieved solely by changing the transport layer. QUIC's independent streams prevent head-of-line blocking from halting the broker queue, maintaining steady message drain.
* **MQTT TCP at $N=20$:** Reaches a median latency of **95.9 seconds** with a 1.4\% loss rate. The queue buildup under TCP retransmission congestion is catastrophic.
* **MQTT-QUIC Client Deadlock ($N=20$):** At $N=20$ degraded 10~Hz, the MQTT-QUIC run was aborted. As trace logs show, after the 30-second send loop completed, the client's call to \texttt{nng\_close(g\_sock)} deadlocked inside the NNG/MsQuic thread cleanup loop. The container hung and was terminated by the runner's 90-second timeout. Because the deadlock occurred *before* the client could write its CSV logs, no data rows were generated.

\section{What this means for CAMPUS}

\begin{table}[H]

\begin{tabular}{lrrrrl}
\toprule
Protocol & Clean p50 & Normal 5G p50 & Degraded p50 & Msg loss & Scales to $N=20$? \\
\midrule
Zenoh-QUIC  & 0.59~ms & 40~ms & 162~ms       & $<$1\%   & Yes \\
Zenoh (TCP) & 0.61~ms & 40~ms & 163~ms       & $<$1\%   & Yes \\
MQTT-QUIC   & 3.56~ms & 43~ms & 213~ms       & $<$1\%   & Yes \\
gRPC        & 2.91~ms & 41~ms & 163~ms       & $<$1\%   & No (50\% loss at $N=20$) \\
MQTT (TCP)  & 45~ms   & 106~ms & 34{,}946~ms  & 1--2\%   & No (queue collapse) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The server results confirm our protocol recommendations with higher precision:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Zenoh-over-QUIC is the primary choice for the pub/sub layer.} It offers the lowest overhead ($\sim$0.5~ms baseline), zero message loss at $N=10$, flat latency curves to $N=20$, and robust performance under packet loss.
    \item \textbf{MQTT-over-QUIC is viable if a central broker is required.} Swapping the transport to QUIC keeps latency bounded to **213~ms** under degraded conditions compared to the 35 seconds of MQTT TCP.
    \item \textbf{gRPC is restricted to point-to-point data transfers.} The 50\% message loss at $N=20$ indicates an architectural limitation of HTTP/2 stream concurrency under high fan-out, disqualifying gRPC as a broadcast/group communication protocol.
    \item \textbf{MQTT over TCP is disqualified.} A RTT of 95.9 seconds at $N=20$ degraded represents total system failure.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Limitations and Next Steps}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Physical 5G radio validation:} The next phase will run a subset of this matrix on a physical 5G testbed. We will start with a local 5G SA core using Open5GS + UERANSIM to validate our deployment workflow.
    \item \textbf{QUIC Client socket-close hardening:} We must address the client-side deadlock in NanoSDK's \texttt{nng\_close}. A proposed solution is to reorder the connection lifecycle: sleep \textit{before} connecting (to allow the broker's QUIC listener to bind fully) and connect right before active transmission, keeping the QUIC socket active and preventing teardown races.
    \item \textbf{ARM64 image rebuilds:} Before deploying to the Jetson Orin edge boards, we need to rebuild our protocol binaries (especially the MQTT-QUIC C binaries) for ARM64 using multi-arch Docker builds.
\end{itemize}
